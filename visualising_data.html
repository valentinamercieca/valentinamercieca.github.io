Visualising Data

The Visualising Data module explored how data can be effectively communicated through visual means, combining technical tools with design principles to support analysis and decision-making. Over twelve weeks, I developed skills in R, Python, and Tableau, learning how to apply these in contexts such as exploratory analysis, statistical modelling, and dashboard design.

The module emphasised both practical application and critical evaluation, helping me build confidence in creating visualisations that are not only informative, but also clear, purposeful, and user-focused.

Unit 1: Data Visualisation for Machine Learning

In this first unit, I explored the critical role that data visualisation plays in both data science and machine learning. One of the key takeaways for me was how powerful visuals can be in making sense of complex data. As someone who learns visually, this really resonated with me. Visualisation is not just about making data "look nice" – it’s about discovering patterns, relationships, and insights that might otherwise go unnoticed.

We began by looking at why visualisation is necessary, especially now when data is being generated at an overwhelming pace and scale. Effective visuals can act as a bridge between raw data and human understanding, enabling clearer communication and better decision-making.

I also learned about some fundamental principles that guide good data visualisation – things like clarity, simplicity, and context. A key takeaway from this unit was understanding the three main types of data visualisation:

  • Explanatory – Used to communicate a clear message, combining relevant data, engaging visuals, and a compelling narrative.
  • Exhibitory – Seen in infographics, these visuals present data for interpretation, often depending on the audience's knowledge
  • Exploratory – Used for in-depth analysis, allowing users to query, search, and discover insights within a dataset.

Additionally, while I have mainly used R and Python for data visualisation, this unit introduced Tableau as a new tool. I look forward to trying it out for interactive and business-focused visualisations.

Unit 2: Grammar and Idioms of Data Visualisation

Building on the foundations from the previous unit, this section delved deeper into the principles behind how data visualisations are constructed and interpreted. This week focused on understanding the "grammar" and "idioms" of visualisation – essentially, the rules and patterns that make visual communication effective.

One of the most interesting things I learned was how visualisation has always been a part of human communication – even long before modern tools and technologies. Visuals offer a way to share information without relying on spoken or written language, making them universally powerful.

We explored the basic building blocks that make up a visualisation: things like position, colour, shape, and size. These elements, known as visual encodings, are combined in various ways to represent data. Understanding how to use them effectively is like learning the syntax of a new language.

Another key concept we looked at was data and task abstraction. This involves simplifying complex datasets and aligning them with the goals of the visualisation. It helped me realise that not every chart needs to show every detail; instead, a good visualisation highlights what is important for the task at hand.

This unit enhanced my ability to critically assess different visualisation approaches. I learned to apply structured principles when designing visualisations, ensuring clarity and effectiveness in data representation. Additionally, I gained insights into how different graphical elements – such as marks, channels, and layout – can be manipulated to convey meaning more productively.

Unit 4: Reading, Organising, and Writing Data with R

This unit focused on handling data in R, specifically how to read, structure, and export datasets for analysis. The ability to efficiently import and manipulate data is a crucial skill in data science, as real-world datasets come in various formats and often require cleaning before analysis.

During this unit, I explored:

  • Reading data from different sources, such as CSV files, tab-delimited files, and fixed-width files using functions like read_csv(), read_tsv(), and read_fwf().
  • Organising data by renaming columns, handling missing values, and converting variables into appropriate types (e.g., factors and numerics).
  • Writing data back into files, including CSV, RDS, and Parquet formats.
  • Working with tibbles, a modern alternative to traditional data frames in R.

This unit provided me with a structured approach to data handling in R. The hands-on exercises helped me refine my skills in troubleshooting, data transformation, and file handling, making me more confident in dealing with real-world, messy datasets. Moving forward, I aim to further explore automation in data processing and enhance my efficiency in large-scale data manipulation using R’s tidyverse ecosystem.

Reading Organising and Writing Data with R

Guided by R for Data Science (Wickham, Cetinkaya-Rundel, and Grolemund, 2023), I explored data import, organisation, and export in R. Using read_csv(), I loaded datasets while handling missing values and adjusting options like skip to remove metadata. I also experimented with alternative file formats, such as read_tsv() for tab-separated files and read_fwf() for fixed-width data.

To clean and organise data, I used column renaming (janitor::clean_names()), type conversions (e.g., turning categorical variables into factors), and numeric parsing (parse_number() to fix inconsistent values). I also learned to reshape data efficiently using pivot_longer() and pivot_wider(), which helped structure messy datasets for analysis.

Writing data back into files was another key focus. I compared CSV, RDS, and Parquet formats, understanding their trade-offs in terms of accessibility, efficiency, and compatibility. Additionally, I used problems() to troubleshoot parsing errors, reinforcing the importance of data validation in real-world workflows.

By the end of this unit, I had a stronger grasp of data preparation as a problem-solving process, making me more confident in structuring datasets for analysis and future use.

Unit 6: Visualising Classification Models

This unit focused on using data visualisation to interpret supervised learning models, particularly classification techniques like logistic regression and decision trees. A key theme was understanding how visual tools – such as the confusion matrix, decision boundaries, and ROC curves – can make model behaviour and performance more interpretable and accessible.

I learned how classification models are trained using labelled data and how model performance is evaluated using metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and the area under the ROC curve (AUC). These metrics, when visualised, provide intuitive insight into how well a model distinguishes between classes.

We also explored tree-based methods, such as decision trees, to understand how models split data into meaningful groups. Visualising these trees helped illustrate the decision logic behind each classification, offering transparency in otherwise complex model behaviour.

Classification and Decision Tree Exercises

For this unit, I worked through two Jupyter notebooks: one on logistic regression and another on decision trees. These exercises (James et al., 2021) were kindly shared with me by a colleague during a seminar, which added a collaborative and practical dimension to my learning.

In the logistic regression notebook, I explored how to fit a binary classifier to a dataset and visualise the resulting decision boundary. I also used a confusion matrix to evaluate classification outcomes and created an ROC curve to assess model performance more comprehensively.

In the decision tree exercise, I learned how to construct and visualise decision trees, interpreting how they make splits based on features and thresholds. Visualising the structure of a tree offered clear insights into how individual predictions were made, which is especially useful for non-technical stakeholders.

Reflecting on this, I found that sharing and discussing code with peers deepened my understanding of model performance metrics and the value of visualisation in model interpretation. These collaborative moments often reveal new techniques or perspectives I may not have considered on my own.

Unit 7: Visualising Dimensionality Reduction and Clustering Models

This unit focused on unsupervised learning, where we analyse datasets without predefined labels or outcomes. The main goal was to understand relationships between variables or observations using techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction, and K-means and Hierarchical Clustering for identifying groups within the data.

High-dimensional data is often difficult to interpret visually, so reducing it to two or three principal components allows us to explore its structure more intuitively. Clustering models then help reveal hidden groupings or patterns within this simplified space. Together, these techniques support better understanding, segmentation, and decision-making without needing a target variable.

This unit deepened my understanding of how unsupervised learning can be used to explore structure in data. With PCA, I learned how to reduce the number of variables while preserving the variance in the data, and how to interpret loading plots and scores plots to understand relationships between observations and variables.

I also worked with clustering techniques, including K-means and Hierarchical clustering, to identify groups within datasets. Visualising these clusters – especially after PCA transformation – helped make sense of complex data by highlighting patterns and similarities that would otherwise be hidden in higher-dimensional space.

Additionally, I appreciated how cluster visualisation provides an accessible, visual way to explore grouping and segmentation.

PCA and Clustering in R

For this unit, I worked through James et al.’s (2021) practical exercises using R Markdown, which focused on applying PCA and clustering techniques to high-dimensional data. Compared to the previous unit’s exercises, I found these tasks more challenging, particularly because unsupervised learning lacks the guidance of a target variable. This meant I had to rely more heavily on interpretation, exploration, and visual cues to understand the structure of the data.

The exercise began with PCA, where I scaled the data, interpreted scree plots, and visualised the principal components to observe how data points were spread in lower dimensions. This helped me identify potential patterns and relationships within the dataset.

I then applied K-means clustering, experimenting with different values of k and visualising the resulting clusters. I followed this with Hierarchical clustering, using dendrograms to see how observations grouped together at different levels. Comparing the two methods helped highlight the nuances of each approach and reinforced the value of visualisation in evaluating cluster quality and coherence.

Completing these exercises helped solidify the importance of dimensionality reduction as a preprocessing step, especially when followed by clustering, and demonstrated how vital visualisation is when working with abstract, high-dimensional data.

Unit 10: Introduction to Tableau

This unit introduced Tableau, a widely used data visualisation tool in the business intelligence space (Batt et al., 2020). Unlike programming-heavy tools like R or Python, Tableau offers a more visual, drag-and-drop approach to data analysis, making it accessible to users with varying levels of technical expertise.

I learned how to connect Tableau to different data sources, navigate its interface, and build a variety of visualisations, including bar charts, line graphs, and scatterplots. The unit also explored how to apply filters, calculated fields, and interactive dashboards to gain deeper insights and enhance user engagement.

From a business perspective, Tableau stood out as a tool for rapid prototyping and real-time data exploration, especially in decision-making environments where time and clarity are critical. Its built-in analytics features – like trend lines, forecasts, and clustering – also helped reinforce key concepts in data storytelling and business-focused analysis.

Tableau Visualisation Practice

For the practical part of this unit, I explored Tableau by connecting to sample datasets and creating interactive dashboards and visual analytics. I experimented with dragging fields into different shelves to create quick visual summaries, as well as applying filters and parameters to make the charts more dynamic.

Compared to R and Python, I appreciated how quickly I could generate insights visually in Tableau. It allowed me to focus more on design and interpretation, rather than syntax. This hands-on experience made it clear how Tableau fits into a broader toolkit for data-driven decision making, especially in a business setting.

While Tableau’s simplicity is a strength, it also made me think more about design discipline – choosing the right chart types, using colour meaningfully, and ensuring interactivity added value. Overall, this unit helped me gain confidence in using Tableau and showed me how it can complement my existing skills in R and Python.
