Research Methods and Professional Practice

The Research Methods and Professional Practice module offered both practical and theoretical grounding in computing research. Over twelve weeks, I explored core topics including the scientific method, research design, data collection and analysis, alongside the ethical, legal, and professional responsibilities in technology. These insights equipped me to plan, evaluate, and conduct research; laying a foundation for my MSc capstone project. Weekly tasks, case studies, and milestone activities helped me critically engage with research strategies and reflect on professional practice in both academic and real-world contexts.

Unit 1: Introduction to Research Methods and Ethics in Computing

This unit introduced foundational research concepts, including the scientific method and inductive versus deductive reasoning (Dawson, 2015; QuestionPro, 2021). Understanding how observations or theories shape research helped clarify how questions and approaches are formed.

We also examined ethics in computing, particularly when dealing with people or sensitive data. The Menlo Report added legal and public interest considerations to traditional ethical principles (Finn and Shilton, 2023), which felt especially relevant in the age of AI. Deckard (2023) raised timely concerns around bias, privacy, and accountability in generative AI. The seminar discussions on grading and project ideas set the module’s tone, balancing planning with critical reflection.

Collaborative Learning Discussion 1: Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct (Initial Post)

I analysed the Automated Active Response Weaponry case through the ACM and BCS codes of conduct, focusing on ethical challenges in autonomous military technologies. My post addressed obligations to avoid harm, protect public interest, and maintain accountability (ACM, 2018; BCS, 2022). I also explored whistleblowing as a professional safeguard and examined tensions between legal duties and societal impact (Culiberg & Mihelič, 2017; ICRC, 2018). This exercise reinforced the importance of applying professional codes to complex ethical dilemmas in computing.

Unit 3: Methodology and Research Methods

This unit clarified how research methods align with philosophical assumptions and project goals. Drawing on Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill (2019), I explored how epistemology and ontology inform methodological choices. The distinction between exploratory and conclusive designs helped assess when qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods are appropriate. While primary data collection may not suit my topic on suspect profiling, this unit helped me evaluate feasible and ethical alternatives using secondary data.

Unit 4: Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

This unit enhanced my understanding of qualitative methods such as case studies, focus groups, and observational research. Their strength lies in exploring “why” and “how” questions in context-rich environments (Kaplan and Maxwell, 2005), though I also noted their challenges with bias and limited generalisability (Priya, 2020; Wohlin, 2021). The emphasis on research trustworthiness through triangulation and reflexivity (Hoepfl, 1997) reinforced the need for ethical, rigorous qualitative design in computing research.

Unit 5: Interviews, Survey Methods, and Questionnaire Design

This unit examined the effective use of interviews, surveys, and questionnaires in research. I learned that interviews offer in-depth qualitative insight, while structured questionnaires enable large-scale data collection. Key lessons included designing clear, unbiased questions, balancing open and closed formats, and piloting for validity (Dawson, 2015). Ethical considerations such as anonymity and fatigue were also emphasised, alongside analysis tools like NVivo and Excel (Allsop et al., 2022; Learning for Action, no date). These insights improved my confidence in selecting data collection tools aligned to specific research goals.

Unit 6: Quantitative Methods - Descriptive and Inferential Statistics

This unit revisited key principles of quantitative research, offering a valuable refresher on descriptive statistics for summarising and interpreting numerical data. While I was already familiar with core concepts such as levels of measurement, mean, median, standard deviation, and interquartile range, the structured content reinforced their application within a research context. The reading by Berenson, Levine and Szabat (2019) emphasised clarity, objectivity, and ethical reporting, further sharpening my approach to statistical analysis and interpretation.

Unit 7: Inferential Statistics and Hypothesis Testing

Unit 7 revisited core statistical concepts I was already familiar with, offering a valuable opportunity to consolidate and apply my understanding of inferential statistics. Topics such as probability distributions, p-values, and hypothesis testing frameworks – including Type I and Type II errors – were reinforced through structured activities (Berenson, Levine and Szabat, 2019). While not new, engaging with these concepts in the context of computing research helped me reflect more critically on how and when to generalise findings from sample data (Purdue University, 2023).

Unit 9: Validity and Generalisability in Research

This unit reinforced the importance of validity, reliability, and generalisability in designing and evaluating research. I reflected on how reliability ensures consistency, while validity confirms the accuracy of what is being measured. Generalisability helped me appreciate how findings can (or cannot) be extended to broader contexts. I also revisited threats to research quality – such as bias, chance, and confounding – and reflected on how these apply differently to qualitative and quantitative methods. These principles now shape how I plan and critique research design.
